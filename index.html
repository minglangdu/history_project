1778 - The Culper Spy Ring

The Culper Spy Ring was organized in New York to gather information about the regulars' activities during the Revolutionary War. Its leader, Major Benjamin Tallmadge, came up with the idea of using a codebook as a substitution cipher, storing 763 numbers mapping to names, places, and words. Unfortunately, numbers 277, 278, 279, and 617 are damaged and unreadable.

1804-1806: Lewis and Clark

Lewis and Clark, by the suggestion of Thomas Jefferson, sent messages encoded in the Vigenère cipher, which was a significant improvement from the pure substitution of the Tallmadge codes. This cipher was based on an algorithm, not a codebook, and thus was convenient. It only required a Vigenère tableau and the knowledge of a single codeword to encrypt and decrypt text.

1861-1865: The Civil War

The Route Cipher

Anson Stager created the route cipher during the civil war to be an easily-relayed yet strong cipher. While it seemed weak since it kept the words intact, the route cipher's combination of substitution, transposition, and dummy words made it extremely difficult to break. Additionally, since people can transmit words better than letters, there was little loss of meaning due to the "broken telephone effect."

Note: I will be using a preset grid and route to encrypt the messages, but the Union used multiple grid configurations and routes in their ciphers. I also will not be implementing dummy words or substitutions, but the Union also used them.

The Rail-Fence Cipher

The rail-fence cipher was a weak cipher that was used in the civil war by both sides, and was inferior to the route cipher in many ways. It was hard to communicate since it scrambled the words, causing many ciphertexts to be so corrupted by human error that decrypting them would yield gibberish. Additionally, it only used one weak transposition to encrypt plaintext, unlike the route cipher's synergy between transposition and substitution.

Note: While the Rail-fence cipher could potentially have multiple rails, I am assuming that only two rails are used to put the characters on.

1918-1932: The Enigma

First patented and created at the end of WWI for commercial purposes, the Enigma would later be used in WWII to encrypt critical messages sent by Nazi Germany. Both the invention itself and the decryption of its codes were significant milestones for cryptography, greatly advancing the field's discovery. The enigma used a keyboard for input, a lampboard for output, rotors to scramble the text, and a plugboard to substitute some letters for others on the keyboard and lampboard.

Note: the plugboard is not implemented in this demo, only 3 rotors are used, and it is assumed that they start at position 'AAA.'

1976-2025: Public Key Cryptography

Public key cryptography is a breakthrough in cryptography that is still being used today. This kind of cryptography uses not one but two keys to encrypt and decrypt text, respectively: the titular public key and a private key. Public key cryptography refers not to only one algorithm but many different applications such as digital signatures, encryption of messages, and the Diffie-Hellman key exchange.

Unfortunately, there is no implementation of public key cryptography in this history project because it is outside the skill of the presenter to implement it. Further research could be to find an implementation of this kind of cryptography.
