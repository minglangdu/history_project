History Project

This website contains explanations, demos, and other supplementary material for my (Minglang Du) history project on the history of cryptography in the U.S.


1778 - The Culper Spy Ring

The Culper Spy Ring was organized in New York to gather information about the regulars' activities during the Revolutionary War. Its leader, Major Benjamin Tallmadge, came up with the idea of using a codebook as a substitution cipher, storing 763 numbers mapping to names, places, and words. Unfortunately, numbers 277, 278, 279, and 617 are damaged and unreadable.

A processed (by me) list of these 763 codes can be downloaded here.

1804 - Lewis and Clark

Lewis and Clark, by the suggestion of Thomas Jefferson, sent messages encoded in the Vigenère cipher, which was a significant improvement from the pure substitution of the Tallmadge codes. This cipher was based on an algorithm, not a codebook, and thus was convenient. It only required a Vigenère tableau and the knowledge of a single codeword to encrypt and decrypt text.
