The Tallmadge Codes

This early form of cipher is purely a substitution cipher, a cipher that replaces specific units of plaintext with ciphertext in a defined manner as dictated by a key. Here, the units of plaintext are words, and the key is of course the spy ring codebook.

These codes are very rudimentary and would obviously not work well today since a single intercepted codebook could mean the end of an operation, and it would be inevitable that one codebook was taken. Additionally, these codes are not vulnerable to one major flaw of pure substitution ciphers, frequency analysis. Frequency analysis is essentially finding how frequently some letters (or words) appear in normal text, and comparing that to how frequently the substituted letters appeared.

As a result, these codes are only examples of how cryptography was employed even in the beginning of the Revolutionary War. Comparing their flaws to new ciphers now shows how far we have come in terms of cryptography.
